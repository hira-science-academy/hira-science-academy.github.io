📘 Chapter 1 – Physical Quantities and Measurements ➕

Key Concepts: This chapter introduces physical quantities, their measurement, and the tools used for precise measurements. It covers the distinction between base and derived quantities, the SI system, scientific notation, and common laboratory instruments like Vernier calipers and screw gauges. The chapter also discusses errors, uncertainties, and significant figures in measurements.

Physical vs. Non-Physical Quantities:
• Physical: Measurable (e.g., length, mass).
• Non-Physical: Subjective (e.g., love, fear).
• Measurement Components: Number (magnitude) + Unit.
• SI Base Units: Meter (m), kilogram (kg), second (s), etc.
Base vs. Derived Quantities:
• Base: Fundamental (e.g., length, mass).
• Derived: Expressed in base units (e.g., speed = \( \frac{m}{s} \)).
• Examples:
- Area: \( m^2 \).
- Force: Newton (N) = \( kg \cdot m \cdot s^{-2} \).
Measurement Tools & Precision:
• Vernier Calipers: Measures up to 0.1 mm (L.C = 0.1 mm).
• Screw Gauge: Measures up to 0.01 mm (L.C = 0.01 mm).
• Stopwatch: Least count = 0.1 s (analog) or 0.01 s (digital).
• Zero Error: Corrected by adding/subtracting based on instrument type.
Scientific Notation & Prefixes:
• Expresses large/small numbers as \( a \times 10^n \).
• Prefixes:
- Sub-multiples: milli (m, \( 10^{-3} \)), micro (μ, \( 10^{-6} \)).
- Multiples: kilo (k, \( 10^{3} \)), mega (M, \( 10^{6} \)).
• Example: 5 pm = \( 5 \times 10^{-12} \) m.
Errors & Uncertainties:
• Systematic Errors: Consistent (e.g., zero error).
• Random Errors: Unpredictable (e.g., environmental fluctuations).
• Human Errors: Misreading scales or parallax errors.
• Reduction Methods: Calibration, averaging, using digital tools.
Significant Figures & Rounding:
• Rules:
1. Non-zero digits are significant.
2. Leading zeros are not significant.
3. Trailing zeros after a decimal are significant.
• Rounding:
- If digit after cutoff ≥ 5, round up.
- For 5, round to nearest even number (e.g., 4.45 → 4.4).
Key Definitions:
• Least Count: Smallest measurable increment (e.g., 1 mm for a ruler).
• Parallax Error: Incorrect reading due to angled viewing.
• Precision vs. Accuracy:
- Precision: Consistency in measurements.
- Accuracy: Proximity to true value.
• Mass vs. Weight: Mass (kg) is scalar; weight (N) is a force.

📗 Chapter 2 – Kinematics ➕

Key Concepts: This chapter explores kinematics, the branch of mechanics that studies motion without considering forces. It covers scalar and vector quantities, types of motion, displacement, velocity, acceleration, and graphical analysis of motion. The chapter also introduces the equations of motion and their applications, including free-fall under gravity.

Scalar vs. Vector Quantities:
• Scalar: Magnitude only (e.g., distance, speed).
• Vector: Magnitude + direction (e.g., displacement, velocity).
• Representation: Vectors use arrows (e.g., \(\overrightarrow{v}\)).
• Addition: Head-to-tail rule for vectors; scalars add algebraically.
Types of Motion:
• Translatory: Linear, circular, or random paths.
• Rotatory: Fixed-axis rotation (e.g., spinning top).
• Vibratory: To-and-fro motion (e.g., pendulum).
• Relative Motion: Rest/motion depends on the observer's frame.
Displacement vs. Distance:
• Distance: Scalar, path length (e.g., 320 km road trip).
• Displacement: Vector, shortest path (e.g., 5 km north).
• SI Unit: Meter (m) for both.
Speed & Velocity:
• Speed (\(v\)): Scalar, \(v = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Time}}\) (unit: \(ms^{-1}\)).
• Velocity (\(\overrightarrow{v}\)): Vector, \(\overrightarrow{v} = \frac{\text{Displacement}}{\text{Time}}\).
• Uniform Velocity: Constant speed + direction (e.g., paratrooper).
• Non-Uniform Velocity: Changing speed/direction (e.g., traffic).
Acceleration:
• Definition: Rate of change of velocity (\(a = \frac{\Delta v}{t}\)).
• Types:
- Positive: Speed increases (e.g., car overtaking).
- Negative (Retardation): Speed decreases (e.g., braking).
• SI Unit: \(ms^{-2}\).
Graphical Analysis:
• Distance-Time Graph:
- Straight line: Uniform speed (slope = speed).
- Upward curve: Acceleration.
- Horizontal line: At rest.
• Speed-Time Graph:
- Straight line: Uniform acceleration (slope = acceleration).
- Area under curve: Distance covered.
Equations of Motion:
• \(v_f = v_i + at\)
• \(S = v_i t + \frac{1}{2} a t^2\)
• \(2aS = v_f^2 - v_i^2\)
• Assumptions: Straight-line motion, uniform acceleration.
Free-Fall & Gravity:
• Gravitational Acceleration (\(g\)): \(9.8\ ms^{-2}\) downward.
• Equations for Free-Fall: Replace \(a\) with \(g\) in motion equations.
• Mass Independence: All objects fall at the same rate in a vacuum.
Key Definitions:
• Resultant Vector: Single vector from vector addition.
• Instantaneous Speed: Speed at a moment (speedometer reading).
• Universal Speed Limit: Speed of light (\(3 \times 10^8\ ms^{-1}\)).
• Projectile Motion: Combines horizontal (constant speed) and vertical (accelerated) motion.

📙 Chapter 3 – Dynamics ➕

Key Concepts: This chapter explores the principles of dynamics, focusing on forces and their effects on motion. It distinguishes between contact forces (friction, tension, normal force) and non-contact forces (gravity, electromagnetism), while introducing the four fundamental forces (gravitational, electromagnetic, strong nuclear, weak nuclear). Newton's Laws of Motion are analyzed in depth: inertia (1st Law), \( \mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a} \) (2nd Law), and action-reaction pairs (3rd Law). Practical applications include momentum conservation, friction analysis, free-body diagrams, and methods to reduce friction.

Newton's Laws of Motion:
• 1st Law: \( \sum \mathbf{F} = 0 \Rightarrow \mathbf{a} = 0 \) (Law of Inertia)
• 2nd Law: \( \mathbf{F}_{\text{net}} = m\mathbf{a} \) (\( \text{N} = \text{kg} \cdot \text{ms}^{-2} \))
• 3rd Law: \( \mathbf{F}_{AB} = -\mathbf{F}_{BA} \) (Action-Reaction)
• Momentum: \( \mathbf{p} = m\mathbf{v} \) (\( \text{kg} \cdot \text{ms}^{-1} \))
• Impulse: \( \mathbf{J} = \mathbf{F} \Delta t = \Delta \mathbf{p} \) (\( \text{N} \cdot \text{s} \))
• Conservation of Momentum: \( m_1\mathbf{v}_1 + m_2\mathbf{v}_2 = m_1\mathbf{v}_1' + m_2\mathbf{v}_2' \)
Forces and Friction:
• Gravitation: \( \mathbf{F} = G\frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2} \) (\( G = 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \, \text{Nm}^2\text{kg}^{-2} \))
• Weight: \( \mathbf{w} = m\mathbf{g} \) (\( g = 9.8 \, \text{ms}^{-2} \))
• Static Friction: \( f_s \leq \mu_s N \)
• Kinetic Friction: \( f_k = \mu_k N \)
• Terminal Velocity: Achieved when \( \text{drag force} = \text{gravitational force} \)
• Rolling Friction \( \ll \) Sliding Friction
Key Definitions:
• Inertia: Resistance to change in motion (1st Law)
• Mass vs. Weight: Mass (scalar, kg) \( \neq \) Weight (vector, \( \text{N} = m\mathbf{g} \))
• Free-Body Diagram: Visual representation of forces acting on an object
• Non-Contact Forces: Act via fields (e.g., gravity, magnetism)
• Electroweak Unification: Weak nuclear + electromagnetic forces (Nobel 1979)

📕 Chapter 4 – Turning Effect of Forces ➕

Key Concepts: This chapter explores the rotational effects of forces, focusing on torque, equilibrium, and center of mass/gravity. It distinguishes between like and unlike parallel forces, introduces the principle of moments, and explains how forces cause rotation in rigid bodies. Practical applications include stability analysis, centripetal force in circular motion, and problem-solving techniques for equilibrium conditions.

Torque and Rotational Dynamics:
• Torque: \( \tau = rF\sin\theta \) (Nm)
• Moment Arm: \( l = r\sin\theta \)
• Principle of Moments: \( \sum \text{Clockwise Torques} = \sum \text{Anticlockwise Torques} \)
• Couple: Two equal/opposite parallel forces (\( \tau = F \times d \))
• Centripetal Force: \( F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r} \)
Equilibrium Conditions:
• Translational: \( \sum F_x = 0 \), \( \sum F_y = 0 \)
• Rotational: \( \sum \tau = 0 \)
• Stable: Returns to original position (e.g., cone on base)
• Unstable: Topples after tilt (e.g., pencil on tip)
• Neutral: Stays in new position (e.g., rolling cylinder)
Center of Mass/Gravity:
• Regular Shapes: Geometric center (e.g., sphere, cube)
• Irregular Shapes: Found by suspension method
• Stability: Improved by lowering CoG or widening base
• Applications: Racing cars (low CoG), balancing toys (CoG below pivot)
Key Definitions:
• Rigid Body: Fixed distance between particles under force
• Line of Action: Path along which force acts
• Resolution of Forces: \( F_x = F\cos\theta \), \( F_y = F\sin\theta \)
• Dynamic Equilibrium: Uniform velocity (e.g., paratrooper)
• Centripetal Force Source: Tension (string), gravity (orbits), friction (dryer)

📔 Chapter 5 – Work and Energy ➕

Key Concepts: This chapter explores the fundamental principles of work, energy, and power. It defines work as force applied over a distance, introduces various forms of energy (kinetic, potential, and mechanical), and explains the conservation of energy. The chapter also covers different energy sources (renewable and non-renewable) and their applications, along with the concept of power and efficiency in energy systems.

Work and Energy:
• Work: \( W = FS\cos\theta \) (J)
• Kinetic Energy: \( E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \)
• Gravitational Potential Energy: \( E_p = mgh \)
• Mechanical Energy: \( E = E_k + E_p \)
• Conservation of Energy: Total energy remains constant (\( E_{\text{initial}} = E_{\text{final}} \))
Power and Efficiency:
• Power: \( P = \frac{W}{t} \) (W)
• Efficiency: \( \eta = \frac{\text{Useful output energy}}{\text{Total input energy}} \times 100\% \)
• Units: 1 kW = 1000 W, 1 MW = \( 10^6 \) W, 1 hp = 746 W
Energy Sources:
• Renewable: Solar, wind, hydro, tidal, geothermal, biomass
• Non-renewable: Fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas), nuclear
• Applications: Electricity generation, heating, transportation
Key Definitions:
• Work: Done when a force causes displacement (\( W = FS \))
• Energy: Capacity to do work (SI unit: joule, J)
• Power: Rate of doing work (SI unit: watt, W)
• Efficiency: Ratio of useful output to total input energy
• Perpetual Machine: Impossible due to energy conservation

📒 Chapter 6 – Properties of Matter ➕

Key Concepts: This chapter explores the mechanical properties of matter, focusing on elasticity, pressure, and fluid mechanics. It covers Hooke's Law, atmospheric and liquid pressure, Pascal's Principle, and practical applications like hydraulic systems. The chapter also explains how pressure varies with depth and area, and its effects in daily life.

Elasticity and Hooke's Law:
• Deforming Force: Changes size/shape of object
• Elasticity: \( F = kx \) (Restores original form)
• Elastic Limit: Maximum force before permanent deformation
• Spring Constant: \( k = \frac{F}{x} \) (Nm⁻¹)
• Force-Extension Graph: Linear within elastic limit
Pressure and Fluids:
• Pressure: \( P = \frac{F}{A} \) (Pa = Nm⁻²)
• Liquid Pressure: \( P = \rho gh \) (Increases with depth)
• Atmospheric Pressure: \( 1.013 \times 10^5 \, \text{Pa} \) at sea level
• Pascal's Law: \( P_1 = P_2 \) (Transmitted equally in fluids)
• Hydraulic Force Multiplier: \( F_2 = F_1 \times \frac{A_2}{A_1} \)
Applications:
• Hydraulic Systems: Presses, car lifts, brakes
• Pressure Tools: Sharp blades, thumb pins
• Barometers: Measure atmospheric pressure (760 mmHg)
• Manometers: Compare gas/fluid pressures
• Animal Adaptations: Elephant feet (low pressure), deer feet (high grip)
Key Definitions:
• Density: \( \rho = \frac{m}{V} \) (kgm⁻³)
• Inelastic Material: Does not restore shape (e.g., clay)
• Elastic Potential Energy: Stored in deformed objects
• Pressure Dependence: Inversely proportional to area
• Weather Forecasting: Uses atmospheric pressure variations

📚 Chapter 7 – Thermal Properties of Matter ➕

Key Concepts: This chapter explores the thermal properties of matter, focusing on temperature, heat transfer, and thermometry. It explains the kinetic molecular theory, states of matter (including plasma), and the principles of temperature measurement using various thermometers. The chapter also covers temperature scales, absolute zero, and the characteristics of effective thermometers.

States of Matter & Temperature:
• Kinetic Theory: Molecular motion defines states (solid, liquid, gas, plasma)
• Temperature: Measures average kinetic energy (\( T_K = T_C + 273 \))
• Heat: Energy transferred due to temperature difference
• Internal Energy: Sum of molecular kinetic + potential energies
• Absolute Zero: \( 0\,K \) (−273.15°C), where molecular motion ceases
Thermometry & Scales:
• Celsius: \( 0°C \) (ice point) to \( 100°C \) (steam point)
• Fahrenheit: \( 32°F \) to \( 212°F \) (\( T_F = 1.8T_C + 32 \))
• Kelvin: \( 273\,K \) to \( 373\,K \) (absolute scale)
• Thermocouple: \( V \propto \Delta T \) (for high-temperature measurements)
• Liquid-in-Glass: Mercury/alcohol expansion (\( \Delta V \propto T \))
Thermometer Characteristics:
• Sensitivity: Detects small \( \Delta T \) (e.g., clinical thermometer: ±0.1°C)
• Range: Mercury (−39°C to 357°C) vs. alcohol (−112°C to 78°C)
• Linearity: Uniform scale markings (even \( \Delta T \) intervals)
• Calibration: Fixed points (ice/steam) ensure accuracy
Key Definitions:
• Plasma: Ionized gas (4th state; conducts electricity)
• Thermometric Property: Physical trait varying with \( T \) (e.g., volume, resistance)
• Heat Flow: Always from higher \( T \) to lower \( T \)
• Mercury Advantages: Uniform expansion, visible, wide range
• Absolute Zero: Theoretical limit of \( 0\,K \) (no negative Kelvin values)

📖 Chapter 8 – Magnetism ➕

Key Concepts: This chapter explores the principles of magnetism, covering magnetic materials, properties of magnets, electromagnetism, and applications in technology. It explains the behavior of magnetic fields, domain theory, and the differences between permanent and temporary magnets, along with their uses in everyday devices.

Magnetic Fundamentals:
• Magnetic Materials: Iron, nickel, cobalt (ferromagnetic)
• Non-Magnetic: Copper, wood, plastic
• Poles: Like poles repel, unlike attract (\( \text{N-N repel, N-S attract} \))
• Magnetic Field: Region around a magnet (\( \vec{B} \))
• Field Lines: Density indicates strength (closer = stronger)
Types of Magnets & Magnetization:
• Permanent: Retain magnetism (e.g., alnico, steel)
• Temporary: Lose magnetism when field is removed (e.g., soft iron)
• Electromagnets: Current-induced (solenoid + iron core)
• Domain Theory: Alignment of electron spins in ferromagnetic materials
• Magnetization Methods: Stroking, solenoid (Right-Hand Grip Rule)
Applications:
• Permanent Magnets: Generators, speakers, door catchers
• Electromagnets: Relays, circuit breakers, Maglev trains
• Recording: Magnetic tapes, hard disks (fringe field)
• Protection: Soft iron shields (high permeability)
Key Definitions:
• Magnetic Field (\( \vec{B} \)): Force region around a magnet
• Domain Theory: Electron spin alignment in ferromagnetic materials
• Paramagnetic: Weakly attracted (Al, Li)
• Diamagnetic: Repelled (Cu, Bi)
• Demagnetization: Heating, hammering, AC current
• Right-Hand Grip Rule: Thumb points to N-pole in solenoids

📙 Chapter 9 – Nature of Science ➕

Key Concepts: This chapter introduces the Nature of Science, covering its definition, main branches, interdisciplinary connections, and the scientific method. It explores the scope of physics in daily life, the relationship between science, technology, and engineering, and foundational principles like falsifiability.

Science Fundamentals:
• Definition: Collective knowledge about natural phenomena, processes, and events.
• Main Branches:
- Biological Science: Study of living things.
- Physical Science: Study of non-living things.
• Natural Philosophy: Historical precursor to modern science.
Physics in Everyday Life:
• Studies matter, energy, space, time, and their interactions.
• Examples:
- Oscillating motion (pendulum).
- Time dilation (relativity).
• Key Theories:
- Relativity: Space-time relationship.
- Quantum Mechanics: Atomic/subatomic behavior.
Branches of Physics:
• Mechanics: Motion and forces (Newton's laws).
• Thermodynamics: Heat and energy transfer.
• Optics: Behavior of light.
• Electromagnetism: Electric and magnetic fields.
• Quantum Mechanics: Atomic particles.
• Relativity: Space-time and gravity.
• Astrophysics/Cosmology: Celestial bodies and universe evolution.
Interdisciplinary Physics:
• Biophysics: Mechanics of biological systems.
• Medical Physics: Imaging (X-rays, MRI).
• Geophysics: Earth's structure (earthquakes).
• Computational Physics: Simulations for complex problems.
• Climate Physics: Atmospheric dynamics (climate change).
Scientific Method:
1. Observation: Collect data (e.g., shadows form in light).
2. Hypothesis: Proposed explanation (light travels straight).
3. Experiment: Test hypothesis (cardboard holes aligned).
4. Theory: Verified explanation (rectilinear propagation).
5. Law: Widely accepted after repeated testing.
• Falsifiability: Theories must be testable/disprovable.
Science vs. Technology vs. Engineering:
• Science: Discovers principles (e.g., thermodynamics).
• Technology: Applies science (e.g., radar, lasers).
• Engineering: Designs solutions (e.g., bridges, software).
• Interdependence: Collaborative innovation (e.g., AI, nanotechnology).
Key Definitions:
• Hypothesis: Testable explanation for observations.
• Falsifiability: Criterion for scientific validity.
• Mass-Energy Equivalence: \( E=mc^2 \) (Einstein).
• Laser Technology: Based on atomic physics principles.
