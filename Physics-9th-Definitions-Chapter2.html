Unit 2: Kinematics

Class 9 Physics | Punjab Board Syllabus 2025

1 Mechanics

Mechanics is the branch of physics that investigates the motion of objects and the forces responsible for changes in their motion. It forms the foundation for understanding how objects behave when subjected to various forces.

2 Kinematics

Kinematics is the study of motion without considering the forces that cause it. It focuses on describing motion through concepts like displacement, velocity, and acceleration, using mathematical equations and graphical representations.

3 Dynamics

Dynamics examines the relationship between motion and the forces that produce it. This branch of mechanics explains why objects move the way they do, based on concepts like Newton's laws of motion.

4 Scalar Quantity

A scalar quantity is completely described by its magnitude alone, without any directional information. Examples include mass (5 kg), temperature (20°C), and time (10 seconds). Scalars follow ordinary arithmetic rules.

5 Vector Quantity

A vector quantity requires both magnitude and direction for complete description. Examples include displacement (5 m east), velocity (20 m/s north), and force (10 N downward). Vectors follow special rules for addition and subtraction.

6 Vector Representation (Symbolic)

Vectors are symbolically represented in textbooks using boldface letters (A, v, F). In handwritten form, they're denoted with an arrow above the symbol (\(\vec{A}\), \(\vec{v}\), \(\vec{F}\)). The magnitude is written as \(|\vec{A}|\) or simply A without the arrow.

7 Vector Representation (Graphical)

Graphically, a vector is depicted as a directed line segment. The length represents magnitude according to a chosen scale, while the arrowhead indicates direction. For example, a 5 N force eastward might be shown as a 5 cm arrow pointing right.

8 Reference Axes & Origin

The reference frame consists of two perpendicular axes: horizontal x-axis (XX') and vertical y-axis (YY'). Their intersection point O is called the origin. These axes provide a coordinate system for precisely describing positions and directions in space.

9 Direction of a Vector

A vector's direction is specified by the angle θ it makes with the positive x-axis, measured counterclockwise. For example, a vector at 30° points northeast, while one at 210° points southwest. This standard convention ensures unambiguous direction description.

10 Resultant Vector

The resultant vector (\(\vec{R}\)) is the vector sum of two or more vectors. It represents the combined effect of all individual vectors. Mathematically, if \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\) are vectors, their resultant is \(\vec{R} = \vec{A} + \vec{B}\).

11 Head-to-Tail Rule

To add vectors graphically using the head-to-tail method: (1) Draw the first vector, (2) Place the tail of the second vector at the head of the first, (3) Continue for all vectors, (4) The resultant is drawn from the tail of the first to the head of the last vector.

12 Rest

An object is at rest when its position remains unchanged relative to its surroundings over time. For instance, a book on a table is at rest with respect to the room, though both are moving with Earth's rotation. Rest is always relative to a chosen reference frame.

13 Motion

Motion occurs when an object's position changes continuously relative to its surroundings. Examples include a car moving on a road or a pendulum swinging. Like rest, motion is relative - passengers may be at rest relative to their train but in motion relative to the ground.

14 Translatory Motion

In translatory motion, all particles of an object move identically along parallel paths. This can be rectilinear (straight line) or curvilinear (curved path). Examples include a sliding block (rectilinear) or a car taking a curve (curvilinear).

15 Rotatory Motion

Rotatory motion involves circular movement about a fixed axis, where different points move through different angles in equal time. Examples include a spinning top, rotating fan blades, or Earth's daily rotation. The axis may be internal or external to the object.

16 Vibratory Motion

Vibratory motion is periodic to-and-fro movement about a central position. Examples include a pendulum, guitar string, or atoms in a solid. This motion typically involves a restoring force that brings the object back to its equilibrium position.

17 Linear Motion

Linear motion occurs when an object moves along a straight path. Examples include a car moving on a straight road or an object falling vertically. This is the simplest type of motion, described by equations like \(s = vt\) for uniform motion.

18 Random Motion

Random motion describes unpredictable movement with no fixed pattern. Examples include the motion of gas molecules (Brownian motion), a fly's flight path, or dust particles in air. This motion results from multiple, unpredictable forces acting on the object.

19 Circular Motion

Circular motion involves movement along a circular path at constant or varying speed. Examples include a satellite orbiting Earth or a ball on a string being swung. Even at constant speed, the direction continuously changes, resulting in centripetal acceleration.

20 Distance

Distance is the total length of the path traveled by an object, regardless of direction. It's a scalar quantity measured in meters (m). For example, if a car travels 5 km north then 3 km south, the total distance is 8 km, though displacement is only 2 km north.

21 Displacement

Displacement (\(\vec{d}\)) is the straight-line distance from initial to final position with direction. It's a vector quantity (meters with direction). Mathematically, \(\vec{d} = \vec{r}_f - \vec{r}_i\), where \(\vec{r}_f\) and \(\vec{r}_i\) are final and initial position vectors.

22 Position

Position (\(\vec{r}\)) describes an object's location relative to a reference point (origin). It's a vector quantity with both magnitude (distance from origin) and direction. In 1D, position can be positive or negative; in 2D, it's specified by coordinates (x,y).

23 Speed

Speed (\(v\)) is the rate of distance covered: \(v = \frac{\text{distance}}{\text{time}}\). It's a scalar (m/s or km/h). Instantaneous speed is the speed at a moment, while average speed is total distance over total time: \(v_{avg} = \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t}\).

24 Instantaneous Speed

Instantaneous speed is the magnitude of velocity at a specific instant, shown by a speedometer. Mathematically, it's the limit of average speed as time interval approaches zero: \(v = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t}\).

25 Average Speed

Average speed is the total distance traveled divided by total time taken: \(v_{avg} = \frac{s_{total}}{t_{total}}\). Unlike instantaneous speed, it doesn't reflect speed variations during the journey. A car trip with varying speeds has one average speed for the entire trip.

26 Velocity

Velocity (\(\vec{v}\)) is the rate of displacement: \(\vec{v} = \frac{\vec{d}}{t}\). It's a vector (m/s with direction). Average velocity is displacement over time: \(\vec{v}_{avg} = \frac{\Delta \vec{r}}{\Delta t}\). Instantaneous velocity is the derivative of position with respect to time.

27 Uniform Velocity

An object has uniform velocity when it covers equal displacements in equal time intervals, maintaining constant speed and direction. Graphically, this produces a straight-line position-time graph with constant slope: \(\vec{v} = \text{constant}\).

28 Non-uniform Velocity

Velocity is non-uniform when either speed changes, direction changes, or both. Examples include a car accelerating (speed change), turning (direction change), or doing both. This results in a curved or non-linear position-time graph.

29 Acceleration

Acceleration (\(\vec{a}\)) is the rate of velocity change: \(\vec{a} = \frac{\Delta \vec{v}}{\Delta t}\) (m/s²). Average acceleration is \(\vec{a}_{avg} = \frac{\vec{v}_f - \vec{v}_i}{t}\). Instantaneous acceleration is the derivative of velocity: \(\vec{a} = \frac{d\vec{v}}{dt}\).

30 Positive Acceleration

Positive acceleration occurs when velocity increases in the positive direction or decreases in the negative direction. For example, a car speeding up while moving east (+x direction) or slowing down while moving west (-x direction) both have positive eastward acceleration.

31 Negative Acceleration (Retardation)

Negative acceleration (deceleration) happens when velocity decreases in the positive direction or increases in the negative direction. A car braking while moving east or speeding up while moving west both experience westward acceleration. The term "retardation" specifically refers to slowing down.

32 Uniform Acceleration

Uniform acceleration means constant rate of velocity change, both in magnitude and direction. The velocity-time graph is a straight line with slope \(a\). Equations of motion apply: \(v = u + at\), \(s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2\), \(v^2 = u^2 + 2as\).

33 Non-Uniform Acceleration

Non-uniform acceleration involves changing rate of velocity change. This occurs when either the magnitude or direction of acceleration varies. Examples include circular motion (direction changes) or a rocket burning fuel (magnitude changes). The velocity-time graph is curved.

34 Graph

A graph is a visual representation of the relationship between two physical quantities, with independent variable on x-axis and dependent variable on y-axis. In kinematics, common graphs include position-time, velocity-time, and acceleration-time graphs.

35 Gradient of a Distance-Time Graph

The gradient (slope) of a distance-time graph represents speed. Mathematically, \(\text{gradient} = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t} = v\). A steeper slope indicates higher speed, while zero slope (horizontal line) indicates zero speed (at rest).

36 Gradient of a Speed-Time Graph

The gradient of a speed-time graph gives acceleration: \(a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}\). Positive gradient means positive acceleration; negative gradient indicates deceleration. Zero gradient (horizontal line) represents constant speed (zero acceleration).

37 Area Under Speed-Time Graph

The area between a speed-time graph and the time axis equals distance traveled. For uniform motion, area = rectangle (base × height). For accelerated motion, area = triangle (\(\frac{1}{2}\) × base × height) or trapezoid, depending on the graph shape.

38 Gravitational Acceleration (g)

Gravitational acceleration (\(g\)) is the acceleration of freely falling objects near Earth's surface, approximately \(9.8 \text{ m/s}^2\) downward. It's caused by Earth's gravity and varies slightly with altitude and latitude. All objects in free fall experience the same \(g\) (neglecting air resistance).

39 Universal Speed Limit

The universal speed limit is the speed of light in vacuum (\(c \approx 3 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}\)). According to Einstein's theory of relativity, no object with mass can reach or exceed this speed. As objects approach \(c\), their mass increases, requiring infinite energy to reach \(c\).
